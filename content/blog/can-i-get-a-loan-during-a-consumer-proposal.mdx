If you are asking **can I get a loan during a consumer proposal**, you are almost certainly under real pressure — a bill you did not plan for, a payment you are not sure you can make. The technical answer is "usually yes, you can apply." But that answer can quietly lead you somewhere expensive, because the more useful question mid-proposal is often the opposite one: *how do I get through this without taking on new debt at all?* This guide gives you both — the honest rules on borrowing, and the trustee-first alternative that most articles never mention.

![A person meeting a licensed insolvency trustee to review consumer proposal options instead of taking a new loan](https://images.pexels.com/photos/7681091/pexels-photo-7681091.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## Quick Answer

Yes, you can technically borrow during a proposal — nothing makes it illegal — but most mainstream lenders decline while your proposal is active, and the offers that remain are secured or high-rate. Before you apply, do two things: **talk to your Licensed Insolvency Trustee**, and ask whether **amending your proposal** (lowering or deferring payments) solves the problem more cheaply than a loan. Remember the **$1,000 rule** — do not take on more than about that in new credit without disclosing the proposal — and know that new debt which strains your budget can push you toward a **deemed annulment** if you fall behind. If you have already decided you must borrow, our companion post on [loans with a consumer proposal and bad credit](/blog/loans-with-consumer-proposal-bad-credit) covers the products in detail. This one is about deciding whether to borrow at all.

## Can I Get a Loan During a Consumer Proposal? The Real Answer

Filing a consumer proposal does not strip your right to apply for credit. A proposal is a legally binding deal, filed only through a **Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT)** under the federal *Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act* and overseen by the **Office of the Superintendent of Bankruptcy (OSB)**, in which you repay a portion of your unsecured debt over up to five years. What it does *not* do is forbid new borrowing.

What stops most people is approval, not permission. The moment you file, your proposal is reported with an **R7** rating and a notation that you are repaying through a special arrangement. When a lender pulls your file, that R7 and the proposal note are the first things they see, and prime lenders' systems screen them out automatically. So the practical reality is: banks say no, secured products and specialised subprime lenders may say yes, and everything they offer is priced for risk.

There is also a rule people miss. While your proposal is active, you generally should **not take on more than about $1,000 in new credit without disclosing that you are in a proposal** — and that disclosure is often a condition of the arrangement itself. Hiding it gains you nothing, because any lender who would approve you can see the proposal on your report anyway. Before you apply for anything beyond a token amount, a quick call to your trustee is not optional — it is the safest move you can make.

## The Move Most People Miss: Amend the Proposal Instead of Borrowing

Here is the idea that changes the whole decision. A consumer proposal is not a rigid contract you are stuck with — it has built-in flexibility, and your trustee is the person who unlocks it. If your income has dropped or an unavoidable expense has appeared, **your LIT can sometimes file an amended proposal** for your creditors to consider: a lower monthly payment, a longer term, or a temporary adjustment that fits your new reality.

It is not automatic — your creditors have to accept the change, just as they accepted the original — but for many people it is dramatically better than a loan, because it lowers your outflow *without* adding a new debt or a new interest bill. Compare the two instincts honestly:

- **Borrow to cover the gap.** You add a payment on top of your proposal payment, usually at a high rate, and you now have two obligations competing for the same paycheque.
- **Amend the proposal.** You reduce the payment that is causing the strain, keep your protection intact, and add nothing new to repay.

Before either, look at the non-credit levers too: ask essential billers for extensions, check whether you qualify for benefits or a hardship program, and trim where you can for a month or two. The goal mid-proposal is to protect the payment you already promised — not to bolt a second one beside it. Your trustee can walk you through amendment and hardship options, and the OSB's plain-language pages on [consumer proposals](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/office-superintendent-bankruptcy/en/you-owe-money/you-owe-money-consumer-proposals) explain the framework.

## The Trustee-First Decision Tree

When a cash crunch hits, run it through this order before you fill in any application. It is designed to reach the cheapest safe answer first.

1. **Is this a genuine emergency, or can it wait?** If it can wait, wait — nothing here needs a same-day loan.
2. **Is it a need or a want?** A car you need to keep your job is a need; almost everything else can be delayed or downsized while you are in a proposal.
3. **Can non-credit levers cover it?** Biller extensions, benefits, a short hardship plan, or savings come before borrowing.
4. **Can the proposal be amended?** If the strain is your proposal payment itself, call your LIT about lowering it — an amendment beats a loan almost every time.
5. **Is a secured product the only realistic fit?** If you truly must borrow, a secured option (a card backed by a deposit, or a car loan backed by the vehicle) is safer and cheaper than unsecured subprime.
6. **Have you called your trustee?** Do this before you sign anything over about $1,000. Always.

Working the tree in order is the whole discipline. Most crunches resolve at step three or four — long before a high-interest loan enters the picture.

## What Happens If You Borrow and Can't Keep Up: Annulment

This is the risk that makes borrowing mid-proposal so different from borrowing at other times, and it deserves its own section because the stakes are high.

A consumer proposal can be **deemed annulled** if you fall roughly **three months' worth of payments behind**. Annulment is not a slap on the wrist — it *ends the deal*. You lose the legal protection the proposal gave you, your creditors can resume collecting the **original** debts, and interest that had stopped can start again. In plain terms, you can end up owing close to what you owed before you filed, minus what you paid in, and having lost the arrangement that was fixing it. Reviving or re-filing is possible in some situations but far from guaranteed, and it may push you toward bankruptcy instead.

Now connect the dots: a new high-interest loan is one of the fastest ways to strain a budget that was already set to the edge, and a strained budget is how people fall three payments behind. That is the quiet danger — not the loan's rate alone, but what the loan's payment does to the proposal you are working so hard to finish. If you are already feeling the squeeze, the answer is a call to your trustee, not a new application.

## The Rare Cases Where a Loan Actually Makes Sense

None of this means borrowing during a proposal is never right. There are narrow, legitimate cases — and they share a pattern: the loan is **secured**, it addresses a genuine need, and it does not endanger your proposal payment.

| Situation | Why it can make sense | The guardrail |
|---|---|---|
| Secured car loan to keep working | No car, no income; the vehicle is collateral | A down payment, an affordable payment, trustee sign-off |
| Secured credit card to rebuild | Adds positive history while you finish | Small limit, paid in full monthly |
| Small, disclosed, affordable amount | A true emergency with no cheaper option | Stay under the 35% cap; tell the lender about the proposal |

In each case the point is the same: the loan serves the proposal, it does not compete with it. ## The 35% Cap and the Scams That Target Proposal Filers

If you do shop, know your protections. As of **January 1, 2025**, Canada's criminal rate of interest is capped at **35% APR**; any lender charging more is breaking the law. A legal 35% is still punishing, so treat it as a ceiling, not a target.

People in a proposal are prime targets for fraud, precisely because banks have turned them away. Walk away from **"guaranteed approval, no credit check,"** any **upfront fee** to release a loan, **pressure to sign immediately**, and anyone who tells you to **hide the loan from your trustee**. Our guides on [spotting predatory lenders](/resources/guides/how-to-spot-predatory-lenders) and [avoiding loan scams](/resources/guides/avoiding-loan-scams) show exactly what to check, and the [FCAC](https://www.canada.ca/en/financial-consumer-agency/services/loans.html) publishes plain-language facts on the cost of borrowing.

> This article is general information, not financial or legal advice. Every consumer proposal is different, and your Licensed Insolvency Trustee is the right person to advise on new credit — or an amendment — for your specific situation.

## The Bottom Line

So, **can I get a loan during a consumer proposal?** Technically yes — but "can I" is the wrong question, and answering it in isolation is how people undo their own fresh start. The better sequence is: protect the payment you already made, talk to your trustee, and ask whether **amending the proposal** or a non-credit lever solves the crunch before any loan does. If you genuinely must borrow, keep it secured, small, disclosed, and under the 35% cap — and never let a new payment put you within reach of a deemed annulment.
