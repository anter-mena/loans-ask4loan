Police chiefs and the lending industry rarely deliver the same message, which is exactly why this one is worth hearing. **The Ontario Association of Chiefs of Police (OACP) and the Canadian Lenders Association (CLA) are warning that illegal lenders could fill the gap left by Canada's rate-cap changes** — moving in on borrowers who no longer qualify for regulated credit under the tighter 35% limit. The warning is not an argument that cheaper legal borrowing is bad news. It is a reminder that when the border between legal and illegal lending moves, criminals set up shop right on the line — and knowing exactly where that line now sits is the cheapest financial protection a Canadian borrower can get.

![A person on the phone reviewing a loan offer and checking whether the lender is licensed](https://images.pexels.com/photos/5915242/pexels-photo-5915242.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## What police and lenders are warning about

The concern centres on who gets left behind when the ceiling on legal lending drops. An industry-linked estimate suggests roughly **4.7 million Canadians — about 16% of those with active credit files, mostly non-prime borrowers — could lose access to regulated credit** under the tighter cap. When a licensed lender can no longer serve a higher-risk file at 35% APR, the need behind that file does not vanish. The borrower still wants the money, and someone unlicensed may be very willing to supply it.

Barry Horrobin, co-chair of the OACP's Community Safety and Crime Prevention Committee, put it bluntly: the legislation "has the potential to create a vacuum for criminals to fill."

The appetite for that vacuum is already measurable. Survey data cited in coverage of the warning found that **close to 3 in 10 Canadians who borrowed from alternative lenders had considered illegal loan sharks** — and that was before the cap tightened the regulated market further.

**A note on the numbers.** The CLA is an industry association whose members operate in the very market the cap restricts, and the 4.7-million figure is an industry estimate, not an independent government count. Reasonable people can disagree about how many borrowers will actually be pushed out. But the practical takeaway holds either way: some Canadians will be approached by lenders operating outside the law, and every borrower should be able to tell a legal lender from an illegal one in about five minutes.

### What changed on January 1, 2025

The backdrop is the biggest rewrite of Canada's interest-rate law in more than four decades.

| The rate-cap changes | Detail |
|---|---|
| Effective date | January 1, 2025 |
| Old criminal interest threshold | Equivalent to 48% EAR |
| New maximum allowable rate | 35% APR |
| Payday loan cap | $14 per $100 borrowed |
| Significance | Most significant change to interest-rate law in more than 40 years |

In plain terms: any lender quoting more than 35% APR on a regular loan — or more than $14 per $100 on a payday loan — is outside the law. There is no grey zone and no exemption a salesperson can invoke over the phone. ## The legal-lender checklist

You do not need legal training to verify a lender. Five checks, all doable before you sign anything, separate the regulated market from the criminal one.

| Check | A legal lender | A red flag |
|---|---|---|
| Licensing | Appears in your provincial consumer-protection registry | No licence number, or refuses to name its regulator |
| Rate quoted | At or under 35% APR, or no more than $14 per $100 on a payday loan | Vague about the APR, quotes above the caps, or will not put the rate in writing |
| Upfront fees | Never demands money before advancing the loan | Insurance, processing or release fee required to unlock your funds |
| Address | Has a verifiable business address you can confirm | Operates only through messaging apps, with no fixed location |
| Pressure | Gives you time to read the contract | Pushes you to sign immediately, or hints at consequences if you refuse |

Two of these deserve emphasis. First, the **upfront fee** is the single most reliable tell — no legitimate lender in Canada asks you to pay money to receive money, a pattern we break down in our guide to [avoiding loan scams](/resources/guides/avoiding-loan-scams). Second, the **registry check** takes minutes: every province maintains a public consumer-protection registry of licensed lenders, and a company that is not in it has no business holding your banking details. For the longer list of manipulation tactics that sit just inside — or just outside — the law, see our full guide on [how to spot predatory lenders](/resources/guides/how-to-spot-predatory-lenders), and our breakdown of [payday loan scams in Canada](/blog/payday-loan-scams-canada) for the schemes that target cash-strapped borrowers specifically.

## If you have already dealt with a suspicious lender

If any of the red flags above describe a lender you have spoken to — or borrowed from — act on it rather than sitting on it.

- **Stop paying upfront fees immediately.** If someone is demanding a fee to release your loan, no loan is coming. Every additional payment is simply lost.
- **Report it.** Contact your provincial consumer-protection regulator, and file a report with the **Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre at 1-888-495-8501 or antifraudcentre.ca**. Reports matter even when no money changed hands, because they help police map where illegal lending is spreading.
- **Keep everything.** Ads, texts, e-transfer records, contracts, phone numbers — all of it strengthens a report and any later investigation.
- **Watch for impersonation.** Illegal operators frequently pose as legitimate companies or agencies; our coverage of the [Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre impersonation warning](/news/canadian-anti-fraud-centre-impersonation) shows how convincing those fronts can be.

If you borrowed from an unlicensed lender, remember that you are the person the law is designed to protect. Reporting is how you get help — not how you get in trouble.

## The bottom line

A joint warning from police chiefs and the lending industry is unusual enough to take seriously, even after discounting for the industry's stake in the debate. The 35% cap and the $14-per-$100 payday limit make legal borrowing cheaper for those who qualify — and give every Canadian a bright, simple line for judging any offer: licensed, at or under the cap, no upfront fees, real address, no pressure. Run those five checks before you sign, and start any search from the regulated market — our [loans hub](/loans) compares licensed options by type and province — rather than from whoever messaged you first. The lenders the police are warning about depend on borrowers who feel they have no other door to knock on. Prove them wrong.
