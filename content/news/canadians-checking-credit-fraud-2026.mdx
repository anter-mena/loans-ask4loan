Fraud fear has turned an annual chore into a monthly ritual. **According to TransUnion's Q2 2026 Canada Consumer Pulse Study, released July 22, 40% of Canadians now check their credit report at least monthly — a shift the study links to rising fraud threats.** Four in ten people pulling their own file every month is a genuine change in behaviour, and a welcome one: your credit report is often the first place identity theft leaves a visible trace. But frequency alone is not protection. A monthly glance only helps if you know what to scan and what to do when something looks wrong. Here is what the study found, why the shift is happening now, and the 10-minute routine that makes each check count.

![A person holding a bank card while reviewing account activity on a laptop](https://images.pexels.com/photos/4386158/pexels-photo-4386158.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## What the study found

The monthly-checking figure sits inside a wider picture of households under sustained pressure. The same survey reports that inflation still dominates the list of financial worries, and that many Canadians are adjusting their spending to cope.

| Finding | Share of Canadians surveyed |
|---|---|
| Check their credit report at least monthly | 40% |
| Rank inflation among their top three financial concerns | 86% |
| Say their income is not keeping pace with the cost of living | 50% |
| Are cutting back on discretionary spending | 51% |
| Plan to apply for new credit | 25% |

Read together, the numbers describe a country watching its money closely on every front. When 86% of people put inflation in their top three concerns and half say income is not keeping up, checking your credit file monthly stops looking like paranoia and starts looking like the same instinct that has 51% trimming discretionary spending: control what you can.

The figure that connects all of this to fraud is the last one. One in four Canadians plans to apply for new credit — which means millions of people will be shopping for loans, cards or refinancing while money is tight. That is precisely the borrower fraudsters and predatory operators go looking for.

## Why Canadians checking credit over fraud is a behaviour shift

The study frames the monthly-checking habit as a response to rising fraud threats, and the timing fits what Canadian authorities have been saying. Just this week, Ontario police chiefs and the Canadian Lenders Association [warned that illegal lenders could move in on borrowers](/news/illegal-lenders-warning-canada-2026) squeezed out of the regulated market by the tighter 35% rate cap. The pattern behind both stories is the same: financial pressure creates urgency, urgency creates shortcuts, and shortcuts are where fraud lives.

Vigilance matters most for exactly the group the study identifies — the 25% planning to apply for new credit. A borrower comparing offers under pressure is more likely to hand personal and banking details to an unverified lender, more likely to skim a contract, and more likely to miss the [warning signs of a predatory operator](/resources/guides/how-to-spot-predatory-lenders). Once your details are in the wrong hands, your credit report is where the damage first appears: a hard inquiry you never authorized, an account you never opened. Many of the [payday loan scams circulating in Canada](/blog/payday-loan-scams-canada) start with nothing more than an application form on a fake website.

A monthly credit check will not stop anyone from stealing your information. What it does is shrink the window between the theft and the discovery — and with fraud, that window is everything.

## The 10-minute monthly credit check

Start with the two facts that make this routine painless. First, **checking your own credit file is free in Canada at both national bureaus** — Equifax and TransUnion each offer access to your report at no charge. Second, **looking at your own file is a soft inquiry**: it is not visible to lenders and has no effect on your score, no matter how often you check. There is no trade-off to weigh.

Check both bureaus, because not every lender reports to both — a fraudulent account can sit on one file and never appear on the other. Then work through four scans:

| What to scan | What you are looking for | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Open accounts | Any credit card, loan or line of credit you do not recognize | 3 minutes |
| Hard inquiries | Credit applications you did not authorize | 2 minutes |
| Personal information | Address changes, or name and employer details you did not update | 2 minutes |
| Balances | Amounts that do not match your own statements | 3 minutes |

An unfamiliar account is the loudest alarm — it usually means someone has already opened credit in your name. An unauthorized hard inquiry is the early version of the same problem: someone is trying. An address change you did not make can signal an attempt to redirect your mail before opening accounts behind it. And a balance that does not match your statements can point to a compromised card worth chasing down. For a line-by-line walkthrough of what each section of the file means, our guide to [understanding credit reports](/resources/guides/understanding-credit-reports) covers it in plain language.

## If you find something wrong

Finding an error or a fraud marker is unnerving, but the response is a fixed, three-front process — and none of it requires a lawyer.

1. **Dispute it with the bureau.** Both Equifax and TransUnion have formal dispute processes you can start online. Identify the entry, explain why it is wrong, and attach any supporting documents. If the item appears on both files, dispute it with both bureaus.
2. **Alert the lender.** Contact the company named on the fraudulent account or inquiry and ask for its fraud department. Ask them to close or flag the account, and to confirm in writing that you did not open it. While you are at it, ask the bureaus about placing a fraud alert on your file, which tells lenders to verify your identity before approving new credit.
3. **Report it to the Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre** at **1-888-495-8501** or antifraudcentre.ca. Report even if you lost nothing — CAFC reports help police map fraud patterns across the country. One caution: scammers have impersonated the CAFC itself, as we covered in our report on the [Anti-Fraud Centre impersonation scam](/news/canadian-anti-fraud-centre-impersonation). The real CAFC will not cold-call you demanding payment or personal information — contact it through the number and website above, never through a number someone else gives you.

Keep records of everything: screenshots, dispute confirmation numbers, names and dates from phone calls. Disputes can take weeks to resolve, and a paper trail keeps the burden of proof where it belongs.

## The bottom line

The TransUnion study describes a rational public: squeezed by inflation, careful with spending, and — 40% of it — watching its credit files the way people once watched their bank balances. That vigilance is worth keeping, and worth sharpening. Ten minutes a month across both free bureau reports, four scans, and a clear escalation path if something is wrong: that is the whole routine. It matters most in the months when you are actively shopping for credit — if that is you, start from the regulated market with our [loans hub](/loans) rather than from whoever advertises hardest, and treat every unfamiliar entry on your file as a message worth reading. The fastest-growing fraud defence in Canada is also the cheapest one — four in ten of your neighbours are already using it.
