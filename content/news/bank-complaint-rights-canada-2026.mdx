Here is a number most Canadians never heard about: over the past year, the country's banking regulator pushed financial institutions to reimburse more than **$38 million to over 745,000 consumer and business accounts**. The reason is blunter still — the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada found that banks were *still not consistently following the rules* on fees, disclosure, and consumer protection. If your bank has ever charged you something that felt wrong, that finding is worth your attention, because the system that recovered all that money only works when people use their complaint rights. Most do not.

![A person reviewing bank statements and preparing a complaint about improper charges in Canada](https://images.pexels.com/photos/8867433/pexels-photo-8867433.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## What the regulator found

FCAC supervises federally regulated banks and enforces the consumer-protection framework. Its most recent annual report shows that supervision produced real money back for real people.

| FCAC supervision (latest annual report) | Figure |
|---|---|
| Reimbursed to consumers and businesses | More than **$38 million** |
| Accounts that received money back | Over **745,000** |
| Complaint-handling deadline for banks | **56 calendar days** |
| Revised complaint guideline published | **2026** |

The context matters as much as the totals. These reimbursements were not gifts; they were corrections. FCAC identified places where banks charged or disclosed things improperly and required them to make affected customers whole — often across an entire product line at once. In 2026 the agency also published a **revised guideline on complaint-handling**, a signal that regulators still see room for banks to treat complaints better.

## Your Bank Complaint Rights in Canada, Step by Step

The reason this system recovers money is that every customer has a defined escalation path. Knowing it turns a vague grievance into a process a bank must respond to.

1. **Complain to the bank first, in writing.** Start with the branch or customer service, describe the issue and what you want fixed, and keep a dated record. A paper trail is your strongest asset.
2. **Escalate internally.** If it is not resolved, take it to the bank's internal complaints office. Banks must handle complaints within **56 calendar days**.
3. **Go to the external complaints body.** If 56 days pass or you are unhappy with the outcome, bring it to the **Ombudsman for Banking Services and Investments (OBSI)** — Canada's single external complaints body for banking. It reviews complaints independently, and it is free.
4. **Report conduct to FCAC.** FCAC does not settle individual disputes, but reporting a bank's behaviour feeds the supervision that produced those 745,000 reimbursements.

Crucially, none of this touches your credit. Complaining is a consumer-protection right, not a mark against you — it is not reported to the bureaus and has no effect on your ability to borrow, as our guide to [understanding credit reports](/resources/guides/understanding-credit-reports) confirms.

## The same instinct that spots a scam

The habit that protects you here is the one that protects you everywhere in borrowing: read what you are charged, question what does not add up, and escalate rather than absorb. It is the same scepticism we recommend for [spotting predatory lenders](/resources/guides/how-to-spot-predatory-lenders), [avoiding loan scams](/resources/guides/avoiding-loan-scams), and dodging [payday loan scams](/blog/payday-loan-scams-canada) — the difference is only that here the counterparty is a regulated bank with a formal process you can hold it to.

That is also why keeping records matters so much. A fee you cannot explain, a product you did not agree to, a charge that appeared without notice — each is exactly the kind of issue FCAC's supervision has forced banks to refund. The money went back to people who flagged it, and the framework exists precisely so you do not have to accept an improper charge quietly. If your concern is fraud rather than a fee, our note on [Canada's bank fraud rules](/news/canada-bank-fraud-rules-2026) covers the newer protections there.

## The bottom line

The regulator's message is twofold: Canadian banks are still getting the rules wrong often enough to require **$38 million in reimbursements to 745,000 accounts** — and the system to recover that money depends on customers who complain. You have a clear, free path: raise it with the bank in writing, escalate to its complaints office within the 56-day window, and take it to OBSI or report the conduct to FCAC if it is not fixed. It costs nothing, it does not touch your credit, and it is the only way improper charges get corrected. If a bank has charged you something you cannot explain, treat it the way you would any suspect financial offer — document it and push back.
