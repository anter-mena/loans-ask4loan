**Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre impersonation** is the subject of a July 14 warning after increased reports of criminals posing as the CAFC in bank-investigator and fraud-recovery schemes. The disguise can include copied logos, official contact details and convincing documents. The safe response is to end the contact and verify independently—not to transfer money to “protect” it or pay to recover an earlier loss.

*Published July 20, 2026. This fraud alert summarizes official guidance available on that date. It does not investigate individual contacts or guarantee recovery of funds.*

## Quick answer: how the impersonation works

The [CAFC bulletin republished by Guelph Police](https://www.guelphpolice.ca/news/posts/canadian-anti-fraud-centre-impersonation/) describes two common paths.

In a **bank-investigator scam**, the caller claims an account or card is compromised. The victim may be told to move money, provide codes or help with a secret investigation. In **recovery fraud**, someone who already lost money is contacted again and promised recovery in exchange for an upfront payment or more personal information.

Scammers may insert genuine CAFC names, phone numbers and branding into fake material. Accurate details prove only that the criminal can copy public information.

## Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre impersonation: 6 checks

1. **End the incoming contact.** Do not continue because the caller creates urgency, knows personal details or tells you the matter is confidential.
2. **Find the official channel yourself.** Type the Government of Canada address or use a trusted bookmark. Do not use the number, QR code or link supplied by the caller.
3. **Call the bank separately.** Use the number on the back of the card or inside the official banking app. If possible, use another device or line after a suspicious call.
4. **Never disclose authentication secrets.** Passwords, PINs and one-time codes can let a criminal approve a transaction or add a device.
5. **Do not move money to a “safe account.”** A transfer to protect funds, help an investigation or test an account is a core warning sign.
6. **Reject recovery fees.** A second contact promising guaranteed recovery may be another attempt to exploit the same victim.

Caller ID is not sufficient verification. Names and numbers can be spoofed, and a professional tone is not evidence of authority.

## If money or information was already sent

Act quickly, but do not rely on anyone promising a guaranteed result:

- call the financial institution through a verified number and ask it to secure affected accounts;
- ask whether a pending transfer can be stopped or recalled;
- change exposed passwords from a device you believe is clean;
- preserve texts, emails, call logs, receipts and wallet addresses;
- report to local police and the [Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre](https://centreantifraude-antifraudcentre.ca/report-signalez-eng.htm); and
- review credit files and use available fraud alerts or locks when identity data was exposed.

Ontario readers can review our [credit-lock update](/news/ontario-credit-lock-2026). A lock can reduce some new-account risk, but it cannot reverse an authorized transfer or protect every type of identity misuse.

## Why loan seekers need extra caution

A person urgently looking for credit may already have shared income, employer or banking data across several forms. A fraudster can use that context to sound credible, claim an application was compromised or request a “verification” transfer.

Use the identity and fee checks in our [payday-loan scam guide](/blog/payday-loan-scams-canada). No legitimate loan problem requires a gift card, cryptocurrency payment, remote access to a phone or a transfer to assist a secret investigation.

The government's proposed [bank fraud rules](/news/canada-bank-fraud-rules-2026) do not remove the need to verify contacts. Proposed or new protections should never be read as a guarantee that every disputed transaction will be reimbursed.

## Bottom line

The **Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre impersonation** warning shows why official-looking details are not enough. Stop the interaction, reach the agency and bank through independently verified channels, protect authentication codes and report losses promptly. The most important test is not how convincing the caller sounds—it is whether the claim survives verification after you end the call.
