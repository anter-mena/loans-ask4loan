The debt strain building across Canada now has a number attached to it. **Canadian consumer insolvencies hit a 17-year high in the first quarter of 2026**, with 37,121 people filing — the most in any quarter since 2009, according to Office of the Superintendent of Bankruptcy data. But the headline hides the more useful story: nearly **80% of those filings were consumer proposals**, not bankruptcies. That single fact says a lot about how Canadians are choosing to deal with debt that has finally outrun their income — and what your own options are if you feel the same pressure.

![A person reviewing mounting bills and debt-relief options with an advisor as Canadian insolvencies climb in 2026](https://images.pexels.com/photos/7681091/pexels-photo-7681091.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## Canada's consumer insolvencies: the Q1 2026 numbers

The Canadian Association of Insolvency and Restructuring Professionals (CAIRP) called it what it is: evidence that more households are reaching a breaking point.

| Consumer insolvencies, Q1 2026 (OSB) | Figure |
|---|---|
| Total filings | 37,121 (highest since 2009) |
| Year-over-year change | +8.5% |
| Share that were consumer proposals | ~80% |
| Filings per hour (average) | ~17 |
| Ontario filings | 13,913 (+14.7% YoY) |
| British Columbia filings | 4,234 (+16.2% YoY) |

"The latest consumer insolvency data suggests more Canadians are reaching a financial breaking point," said Wesley Cowan, a Licensed Insolvency Trustee and CAIRP vice-chair. Reporting pegged the average non-mortgage debt tied to a filing near **$43,000** — the kind of balance that becomes unmanageable once higher rates and living costs squeeze a budget from both sides.

## Why proposals, not bankruptcy

The most telling detail is that Canadians are overwhelmingly choosing **consumer proposals** over bankruptcy. A proposal is a legally binding agreement — filed only through a **Licensed Insolvency Trustee** under the federal *Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act* — to repay a portion of your unsecured debt, often 30 to 70 cents on the dollar, over a term of up to five years. Interest on the included debts stops, collection calls end, and you make one fixed monthly payment.

People choose it because it offers control: it can protect assets that a bankruptcy might not, it avoids the surplus-income payments a bankruptcy can require, and it leaves a slightly less severe mark on your credit file. ## What rising insolvencies mean for you

If these numbers feel personal, you are far from alone — and the most important takeaway is to act *before* debt reaches the breaking point, not after.

- **Stop borrowing to cover borrowing.** Using new high-interest credit to make old payments is the pattern that ends in insolvency. Break it early.
- **Consider consolidation.** Rolling several high-rate balances into one lower-cost payment can steady a budget that is wobbling — our [debt-consolidation guide](/resources/guides/debt-consolidation-guide) explains when it helps, and our [debt-consolidation loans](/loans/by-type/debt-consolidation-loans) hub shows the options.
- **Get a real budget on paper.** Knowing your true monthly gap is the first step to closing it.
- **Talk to a professional early.** A non-profit credit counsellor or a Licensed Insolvency Trustee can lay out every option, from budgeting to a proposal, usually free at the first meeting.

### Warning signs you're nearing a breaking point

Insolvency rarely arrives without warning. A few signals are worth taking seriously: making only minimum payments while balances still climb, using one credit card or loan to pay another, borrowing to cover regular bills like rent or groceries, or having lost track of how much you owe in total. If two or more of these describe your last few months, treat it as a prompt to get advice now — while you still have the widest range of options — rather than waiting until a missed payment forces the decision for you.

## The bottom line

The Q1 2026 record is a warning light for Canadian households: debt loads have outpaced what a lot of budgets can carry, and 37,121 people reached the point of filing in just three months. But the fact that most chose a consumer proposal shows there are structured, legal ways through — well short of, and different from, full bankruptcy. If you are feeling the squeeze, the winning move is the early one: stop stacking new debt on old, look at whether consolidation steadies things, and get honest advice before the numbers decide for you. You have more options at the first sign of trouble than at the last.
